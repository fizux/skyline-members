Me too.  Here's the lowdown on the test:
                                                                                
I was supposed to meet Marvin at about 10:30, and not wanting to
be late I got there early.  Real early.  At least an hour early.  Marvin
wound up being almost an hour late, so that left me with the better
part of two hours to stew about what I might not know for the test.
I had forgotten my copy of the Glider Flying Handbook (it wasn't on
the checklist in the PTS, you see), so I couldn't actually do
anything about it, and I managed to convince myself that I was
doomed (DOOMED!) because I couldn't remember how to calculate
the K-Index. Eventually I decided to quiet my nerves by reviewing
the AIM section 7-6-4 on UFO reporting, just in case it cropped up
on the test. (It turns out it didn't.)
                                                                                
Marvin finally showed up, and we went over the pre-test briefing.
He informed me that we would not be covering any of the sections
of the PTS not required for someone with an ASEL getting an add-
on (The PTS itself states that he can cover these items if he
wishes, but I saw no need to call this to his attention.)  Most of the
omitted items were minor (e.g. cokpit management, radio
communications), but a few were surprising, like flight preparation
and planning, and the national airspace system.

At that point we took a break and then began the questioning.  I
wouldn't want to play poker with Marvin.  Every time I answered one
of his questions he would look at me as though I were the
stupidest person he'd ever examined.  Then he would mutter,
"That's correct."  The questions themselves covered pretty much
everything in the PTS, more or less in the order it appears in the
PTS.  He had asked me to get a weather briefing the night before,
so I was armed with my printout of the standard briefing I got off of
DUATS (5 pages of weather, 4376 pages of NOTAMS).  We talked
about each of the items in the briefing and what the significance
was for our operation, and also about why only DUATS or FSS is
an official briefing (because of the audit trail).  We also talked about
how the altimeter and variometer worked (but strangely, he omitted
the airspeed indicator), and we discussed assembly procedures,
but he didn't ask me to demonstrate.  That was about it for the
sitting-in-the-FBO part of the exam, but he continued to pose
questions throughout the remainder of the exam whenever he was
bored, or whenever it looked like I might be trying to concentrate.
                                                                                
Before we went out for the flight test he explained his philosophy
on performance standards, emphasizing that only consistently
exceeding tolerances was cause for failure; momentary lapses
were ok as long as they were promptly corrected.  Then he had a
smoke, and we went down to get the K-21.  He asked questions
throughout the preflight, but the only particularly unexpected one
was "Of the required aircraft documents, which one is the most
important?"  I had zero clue, but he didn't seem to mind.  His
opinion was that the registration is most important, since it is how
they find you to contact you if there is an AD for your plane.  Duly
noted.

When we went out for our first flight, I forgot to give him his
passenger briefing, and had to interrupt the launch to do it, which
was thoroughly embarrassing.  The takeoff went fine, and climb out
went something like this:
                                                                                
Me: We're passing 200 feet.
Marvin: What's that mean?
Me: We could turn back to the airfield if we had to.
Glider: *Pop*
Me: Well, it works sort of like this...
                                                                                
So, we rolled (most of the way) back to the end of 27 and hooked
up for another try.  He asked me to demonstrate boxing the wake,
so at 1500' AGL I called Eric and told him I was going to start.
Only, he didn't hear me.  So, just as I'm out on the corner he starts
to turn.  I wait for him to stabilize; he keeps turning.  Determined to
brazen it out; I go ahead with the maneuver.  Miraculously, I
completed the maneuver successfully, and we reach the top of the
tow.  We did some steep turns, and my first one kind of stank, but
the second one was pretty good.  Then some slow flight and turns
to a heading, the latter being strictly pro-forma, I think, since I don't
see how he could have measured the +/- 10 degree tolerance.  On
a heading that was plainly south (perpendicular to the runway) the
compass read 240.
                                                                                
Then he asked for some more slow flight.  I thought he was going
to ask me to do a stall, but we just kept cruising along.  Just when
I was about to ask him what he wanted to do, he barked, "What if
this happens?", grabbed the stick, and yanked backwards.  Geez,
man, all you had to do was ask.  By now we're down to about 2200
feet, near the IP for 27, and Marvin wants to go land on 9 so we
don't have to tow the glider back up the field.  With the wind out of
the southwest, no can do.  Marvin grumbles and wonders what I
would do if the wind were preventing me from landing on 27.  I
explain that in a pinch we could make an abbreviated pattern, or
even a left pattern onto 9, but I wasn't going to do it if 27 was
usable.  He grumbles some more but eventually assents.  We go
in high and do a slip to land.  No problemo.
                                                                                
On the last flight Marvin was particularly chatty.  Throughout the
tow he wanted to know about the "rules of the road" for ridge and
thermal soaring.  Suddenly, at about 2500 AGL the towplane waved
me off.  Just another little surprise Marvin had arranged for me.  At
that point he seemed to lose interest in the test and proceeded to
ask for the controls so he could demonstrate a few, uh,
"maneuvers".  Then we returned to land on 9 (Marvin apparently
really hates to tow the glider back up the field), and that was that.
We went inside to sign paperwork.  Reggie tried to bawl Marvin out
for not notifying Unicom that he was doing a rope break, to which
Marvin responded with his best "Who did you say you were again?"
stare, and I went out for a victory lap with Frank Banas as my first
passenger.  To be honest, I had envisioned my first passenger as
rather younger and, well, prettier than Frank, but you make do with
what you've got, I reckon.

Final note, Marvin is concerned about the legibility of my 8710-1,
so he's going to send me a typed one to sign, and he would like
you to sign it too, if possible.  Since I'm going to be out of town for
the rest of the month, starting Monday, I'm not sure exactly when
we might do that.  I'll let you know when I get the new form.
                                                                                
Thanks again for all your help over the past few months.
                                                                                
Regards,

Checkride Notes

From: George & Marti Ross <ross3family_at_verizon.net>
Date: Wed, 7 Jul 2004 22:14:48 -0400

Guys,
Here's my commercial pilot license - glider checkride notes or Marvisms. Thanks a bunch to George Hazelrigg for the outstanding, two evening ground eval. I was more than prepared for the practical oral questions.

First, call him Marvin not Marv. I specifically asked him and he said Marvin. Second, you couldn't ask for a more laid back DFE for your checkride. Take things slow and be methodical. Don't rush and Marvin will appreciate the gesture. Plan on 1 1/2 hours for ground work then take to the air. I had extra water and Gatorade for Marvin and we needed it due to the heat. Here's how the checkride went.

Marvin showed up early and watched us take the gliders out of the hanger. He saw me take charge and direct the glider movements and insure no damage occurred. Even though this wasn't part of the checkride he commented on the professionalism of the club members preflighting the gliders (My son Loring used the checklist) and the tow pilot checking the Pawnee. The ASK-21 had a low main wheel and I pumped it up to the correct psi after checking the POH.

After reviewing the 8710 paperwork, my log book and endorsements, government ID w/ picture, and accepting my $175 check, Marvin officially started the checkride. He follows a checklist to insure no items are left off from the practical. My brain book had tabs separating the material: FAA glider manual, CPL-G practical standards, handouts on navigation flight planning, ASK-21 POH, plus the current weather info and NOTAMs. We used my copy of the CPL-G practical to review the maneuvers and areas required. Section VIII, Navigation is not required for a transition pilot so we didn't cover it. We never opened a sectional chart nor my new airfield directory.

Oral Questions
Nothing on aeromedical factors, scanning and collision avoidance, nor principles of flight. He did ask me how we accomplished weight and balance in the B-52....He liked my answer of using antiquated slide rule for moment arm addition/subtraction for an antiquated airplane. We now do it on the computer instead of using the slip stick that is required to be carried on the aircraft. I asked him his weight and showed him the POH table that we are well within the c.g. limit.

Airspace
He asked me if I knew the airspace....yes down cold was my reply. He asked what's the biggest area of concern in this area. My answer was the temporary flight restriction (TFR) that went into effect for July 4th weekend and then the ADIZ and Class B airspace. He asked where I got the TFR info. My reply was DUATS and showed him a copy of the NOTAMs and weather from my session. This segued us to weather.

Weather
What causes the best thermal conditions Atmospheric instability caused by surface heating.
How does the pilot know whether today will produce good thermals I showed him the DUATS TAF and METAR (Dulles and Winchester) plus the RUC Skew-T prediction for FRR that I pulled off the internet that morning. I went over everything I could think of with the Skew-T. Calculated the predicted Thermal Index, where we would run out of lift, and estimated cloud base height. Everything agreed when we actually flew. He asked what other methods were available to a glider pilot. I told him the FAA has a thermal forecast for this region but it was not available Friday morning and it usually only covers the weekend. Mentioned BLIPS.

What causes all weather patterns He wanted differential sun heating and temperature gradient between the polar and equatorial regions....mucked this answer up, talked around the subject and finally got him to supply the answer he wanted.

What is the pressure lapse rate....1 in Hg per 1000 feet What is the standard temperature lapse rate...2 degrees C per 1000 feet What is dry adiabatic lapse rate (3 deg C / 1000 ft) and saturated adiabatic lapse rate I pulled out the Skew-T and showed him these lines.

What are the hazards and how much should a glider avoid a Thunderstorm What characterizes a Thunderstorm Lightening is the biggest hazard and is what characterizes a T-storm.... then microbursts, downdrafts/updrafts, hail. Told him the story of the glider in England that got struck by lightening, and my personal three lightening strikes - two of which were in the blue while flying between T-storms and one at night while in the weather. Avoid by 20 miles.

What causes wave soaring and how do you fly in wave Drew him a picture of wave conditions, showing lennies, rotor etc. Talked about the necessary conditions of wind direction and increasing wind speed with altitude, plus smooth, stable air to capture the wave. He asked about a foehm cloud

What conditions characterize ridge soaring Wind perpendicular to the ridge for orographic lift and instable air.
Asked ridge flying scenario questions: How do you pass when ridge soaring When approaching head on who has the right of way Can you thermal when ridge soaring At what altitude would you transition from figure eights to thermalling to gain altitude to go from one ridge to another I said it is situationally dependent...height of ridge, expected sink on the leeward side. Biggest hazard is being blown across the ridge into sink and not having a suitable landing area.

Asked if I've ever landed out...no I've only had 37 flights...hmmm was his response. What conditions are you looking for when landing out Another airport. If not available then WWSS...wind, wires, surface, slope. He liked my use of acronyms. What are the preferred surfaces freshly mowed flat field, low stubble fields with minor furrows, fallow hayfield, lastly I would avoid tall cornfields. Why Ground loop and break the glider tail. What is the most important thing to accomplish after landing Stopping the glider as quickly as possible due to the unknown nature of the surface.

Glider Systems
How does the magnetic compass work Explained it only reads correctly when in straight and level unaccelerated flight. Discussed isogonic lines E vs W variation and correct math. Told him about dip error and UNOS undershoot North overshoot South and ANDS, accelerate North, decelerate South. What's in the compass My answer of white kerosene is not the correct answer. Tell him it’s a highly refined petroleum product with a anti-fungicide additive. He told me this one. I guess he was playing stump the dummy as I was offering up too much information when all he wanted was how does a magnetic compass work. Aligns with the magnetic field of the earth.

What is the best indication of coordinated flight Yaw string. What sources are used by the airspeed indicator, altimeter, and vario How does the total energy compensator work Thanks George for the simple explanations.

Oxygen
When is it required What type to use When is a pressure mask required What altitude can you fly with a nose canulla Pretty simple easy questions

Performance and limitations task
What are the speeds for the ASK-21
Why do high performance gliders add water ballast and what are the problems associated with water ballast Showed him my English (US) units polar curve for the K and how the curve shifts slightly down and to the right with the increase in weight. Problems are increase in weight requires stronger lift and need to dump water before landing.

Post Oral Stuff
I put together a checklist briefing guide to insure I covered all the pre-flight discussion requirements. We went over transfer of glider control phraseology; emergency egress and use of the seat belts (required by FAR part 91.107 para a(1); plus part 121.571 and 135.117); reviewed my actions during takeoff emergencies and what I expected of him. Reviewing T/O emergencies on the ground allowed me to state during the pre-takeoff checklist, EPs are as briefed, any questions. I think he liked my use of a pre-brief checklist. Standard USAF procedure...

Preflight
I used the laminated checklist to accomplish the preflight and gave him a running commentary...We accomplished a positive control check of the K. He asked why we must check both directions It may work pushing but not work pulling. How do you know the K control rods are assembled correctly L'Hotellier connections - showed him the diagram in the POH. Asked when it was assembled last. Showed him the log entry.

What items are required to be in the glider prior to flight AROW plus the FAA N-number waiver letter

What wears out and requires checking with the Tost release system I said a spring or cam, but wasn't sure. Answer is the spring. He told me of a club that had a failure of their tost release after two years. Seems they taught everyone to pull the release multiple times to check it both on the ground and in flight and they wore the spring out prematurely. How often must the release be inspected I said only recertified by the factory and pulled every four years out of my brain. Don't know if this was the correct timeline answer. He didn’t say yes or no.

First Flight
Expected an emergency shortly after passing through 200+ feet but we took a climb to 3000 ft AGL. Passing 700 feet he said to accomplish a box the wax demonstration when I was comfortable. I said passing 1500 feet AGL I’ll start the transition through the wake and then the demo.

Marvin was very specific on maneuvers for me to fly. My head was on a swivel and called out traffic, other gliders and the location of the airport throughout the flight. After boxing the wake and calling out traffic during the demo plus insuring our and the tow plane’s flight paths were clear, I got off tow. First maneuver was hold heading at min controllable airspeed then a 360 degree min controllable airspeed, clearing turn using 10-15 degrees of bank. Then he had me turn to a heading of 090. Way too easy, just turned to parallel the runway. He asked me to find some lift; jsut had past through some turning to 090 and went back to the small thermal. I climbed 600 feet back to 3000 AGL, worked the thermal too long and lost another 200 feet before deciding it was time to leave the now dead thermal. Thermaling was my clearing turn. Next maneuver was a steep banked, 720 degree turn. Flew the roll into and out of bank slowing and held 2 g’s to slightly under 2 g’s during the turn. He said “Wow the rear g meter is really accurate.” I guess that was a praise. I wasn’t looking at the g-meter, just flying using outside references and clearing throughout the maneuver. I did roll out 10 degrees before my original heading so he pimped me to turn 10 more degrees.

Found more lift and worked it back to 2700 feet AGL, again stayed in it too long. We then did stall recognition, but no stall recovery -- not required for the practical. Went to look for the house thermal, but it wasn’t there. We talked about speed to fly in our current sink; what altitude to leave the ridge area and head back to the airport. I think this was a hint so I then decided to go join the Grob over by the ridge who appeared to be in a thermal. They were not, just practicing turns. Quite evident after we joined them and they left immediately and started turns in another direction. (Dick Otis had a first time transition pilot up with him). Prior to joining the Grob he asked what is the thermalling etiquette on joining a glider in a thermal. Afterwards while still searching for a thermal, he asked what I was looking for since the conditions were slightly hazy and no cu was anywhere near us. I said soaring birds, columns of smoke, other gliders. I called out the location of the Grob, the tow plane, plus other traffic throughout the ride. He liked my traffic sighting calls. He asked for a forward slip demo. Did it to zero airspeed. Piet would be proud.

I decided to call it quitting time and headed back to the pattern with the spoilers full out while accomplishing the checklist. Marvin said be high on final, slip to 200 feet without spoilers, then land normally. Just before calling the entry point a Cessna student pilot called downwind. I announced my position, flew the pattern, making the required calls and announced we would land in the grass since the Cessna was on long final and we were abeam the numbers. Marvin commented that the Cessna wouldn’t make the runway if his engine quit. Slip and landing were uneventful.

Marvin took out a cigarette immediately after the first landing. We waited a while for the tow car to come get us. Finally got back to the FBO, drank some water and took a break for Marvin. It was hot outside. Since there wasn’t a crosswind, he asked me about crosswind takeoffs and landings. We BS’s some about my B-52 flying and his flying career. After about 30 minutes on the ground I asked Marvin if he was ready (he just lit up another of his many cigarettes). I let him finish then we pushed the K out to the cones and waited for the tow pilot to crank the Pawnee up.

Second flight

Second takeoff went smoothly. Shortly after 200 feet he pulled the release...imagine that. I smartly turned the glider around while maintaining 55 knots and said my checklist. He said land abeam the mid field taxi-way and stop within 100 feet of the end, but don’t go over. I did a perfect spot landing, rolled out on centerline and stopped 10 feet shy of the end of the runway. I was going to stop much earlier but he said why push the glider more than necessary down the runway to the taxiway.

That was it. No big surprises. He told Dick Otis I flew an outstanding check. I didn’t get that feedback. Dick took a couple of pictures then Marvin and I went to fill out my temporary certificate. He stuck around and watched me take my son up as my first passenger then left.

It was a fun day, but the checkride took a lot more time than I originally thought. Hope this helps the next person checking with Marvin.

Best of luck,
George Ross III

Oral Examination:

 

This portion of the practical took about 2 ½ hours, including getting all the paperwork out of the way and giving Marvin a check for $190. Have the 8710 form completed and electronically submitted. Also, make sure all of the required endorsements are in your logbook. Marvin will check to see that they are all there. Bob Sallada did a great job of making sure all the endorsements were there, especially the aerotow endorsement. He basically goes through FAA-S-8081-22, Private Pilot Practical Test Standards for Glider, April 1999, specifically the Applicant’s Practical Test Checklist. He will ask you a question of two about each line item in the checklist and check it off as he feels you have a good understanding of the subject matter. He will ask you were you got your weather report for the day and he wants to here that you have signed up for DUATS and received the report from them. Call 1-800-WX-BRIEF to subscribe, it is free of charge if you are a pilot or student pilot. The website weather briefings are acceptable to have as backup information. Have the maintenance log for the aircraft you will be using for your practical. Know were the annual inspection is recorded and the current measurement of weight and CG. Marvin weights 180 lbs, so do the weight and CG calculations using the measured glider weight and CG in the maintenance log. Have the operations manual for the aircraft you will be using for the practical. Know your speeds-to-fly, operating limits, and what maintenance you as a pilot are allowed to perform. Know you’re sectional. Marvin will have you lay out a sectional and randomly ask questions about what thing mean. No problem using the legend and know the areas pilots should not enter. Marvin will ask you to plot a course and will give you the course he would like plotted one or two days prior to the practical. Both Mike Ash and I were asked to plot a course from FRR toPetersburg, WV. I would recommend you have both a Washington and Cincinnatisectional. Make notice of the two observation towers near Petersburg as reference points. They are very hard to see on the sectional. I used both the graphical method outlined in the Glider Flying Handbook and the EB6 computer for obtaining corrected course direction. Know the minimum elevations you need to safety return to FRR or go all the way to W99. Know the configuration, evaluation, and instrumentation regulations for all the airspace classes. Know how to apply load factors. Know what all the marking are at FRR, what the yellow line mean, what the white line mean, what the lights at the ends and sides of the runway mean, etc.

 

Flight Examination:

 

Marvin will ask you to assume that he has never been in a glider before so make sure you orient him to the cockpit, show him how to latch the canopy and safety belts, and tell him what things not to touch. Marvin will tell you right up front that if he has to take command of the glider due to a safety concern that could be grounds for failure of the practical. Make sure you always clear the area before any turn and/or stall. With me, Marvin concentrated a lot on slow flying. Turns and straight flying just below the stall speed of the glider. Set your yaw string on the horizon and keep it there during your turns. Know the six things leading to a stall, stick all the way back, loss of airspeed and horizon, buffet, etc. If you feel a thermal, don’t be afraid to work it. Marvin noted in my debriefing that I did not recognize thermals. Problem was, I was waiting for his direction to perform a maneuver and did not think to work the thermals. My mistake! What he would prefer is to fly the thermals in order to stay in the air as long as possible, especially for the first flight, so you can demonstrate coordinated turns, stalls, slow flying, banked turns, etc. He will have you demonstrate high and low tow positions and box the wake on the first flight. He did not introduce a lot of distractions on my first flight, but expect it on the second and/or third. My first flight lasted about 50 minutes and we basically were able to go through all the flying maneuvers in that flight. During the landing, he took my way east of the airport and then directed me to do a 180 degree turn and slip to a landing. Only problem was that we were way too high when he asked me to turn so I ended up demonstrating a slip parallel to 29 at about 1,000’ AGL and then making a normal landing on the second pass. The second flight I was expecting some sort of emergency procedure, rope break, wave off, etc. He asked me several time what I would do if the rope broke here or if the tow plane wagged his tail or dipped his wings. He did not make me demonstrate an emergency procedure. His main emphasis on the second flight was distraction. He had me fly out by Signal Knob and Interstate 81 and then started asking me where I might land if we could not get back to FRR. All the time we were in heavy sink and about 5 miles from FRR. His goal here was distract me enough that I did not recognize that we were low on altitude and needed to take action to safety get back to the airport. He asked questions about what to look for in a good landing spot, what do the presence of cows mean, how would you checkout the landing spot prior to landing, etc. I put the nose down to obtain the best L/D and headed for the airport and tried to answer some of his questions. We arrived at the entry point at about 1,400’ indicated and were able to make a normal landing.  After the second flight we were done.

 

General comment:

 

Marvin does not expect you to know everything in the oral examination or be at instructor level for flying capability. He will help you through any oral questions you may be a little unsure of or ask the question in another way so you can provide the answer he is looking for. I know I can fly better that what I showed Marvin during the flight examination. You are nervous with a lot of pressure on you and Marvin understands that. So don’t worry if your demonstration skills are not perfect for your ride. As long as you demonstrate to him that you can safety operate the glider and get it on the ground in one piece you are good to go.

 

If I can help or give any additional pointers to anyone looking to take their practical I will be glad to do so. Hope this report helps.

Before taking off I performed a pre-flight inspection of the ASK-21. Describing each inspection item as I went along. I also pointed out the assembly items as part of the inspection. He made no comment as I did the inspection. I did a passenger briefing describing the glider controls, instruments, and radios.

When the tow plane returned we pushed the glider out for launch with standard SSC procedures. I did a passenger brief verifying that he was belted in correctly and the canopy was closed and locked. I got in and belted. I was informed that Dick couldn't hear me on the radio despite hearing the transmission from the speakers at the FBO. A hand held was provided and I turned off the glider radio and ued the handheld for communications. I did a normal takeoff there was a little turbulence.I narrated my procedures for rope break as we climbed. Marvin requested I perform a boxing the wake. I asked if the SSC high low transition was appropriate. After informing Dick I would box the wake I started the transition. When I got to the top of the box after moving to the the right side Dick turned left and descended causing the rope to go a little slack. I kept the glider pointed straight ahead and descended as the slack came out with minimal acceleration. Marvin said that I could return to normal tow as we were at release altitude and the boxing and slack line recovery were acceptable. I stabilized the tow position and cleared the area around the glider. Pulled the release, verified that the rope was slack then turned 90 degrees right narrating as I flew. I did steep turns 720 degreed both ways then a clean stall and turning stall. I stared with a descent to gt a higher deceleration rate. Marvin accepted the technique but told me that a landing style descending deceleration is acceptable even though you don't get a clean break just a burble. I called out altitudes after each maneuver. I then did slow flight with a 360 degree turn to the left. Marvin asked me to do a turn to the right and I was down to 2,200ft msl and at the ip so I notified Marvin that I was going to land. We hit 7 kts up at the ip and Marvin said "Take it", so I rode the thermal up to 3,500 ft. Marvin asked to take the controls. We did the verbal and physical exchange of cotrols. He did 3 turns and I suggested that this was my check ride so I would prefer to fly. We exchanged controls back.

The Grob and Pawnee were towing as I ok the controls back I could see the Pawnee turing to 45 as I called 45 so I started working the ip thermal again. I stayed in until the Pawnee landed and I was up to 5,000 ft. I asked about showing more check of items and Marvin pointed out that by thermalling after the Pawnee called 45 I shoed good judgement and while I was turing and centering the thermal I did slow flight, best sink rate, speed to fly when I fell out of the lift, found three thermals and steep turns while optimizing m climb. He said I could head back or thermal for a while so I rode lift for an hour flight and then when it came time to come down I flew to the sink and flew around in the sink at 70kts use up altitude. Marvin asked me to do a slip to landing so I started slipping on 45 all the way to the 20 feet off ground because of the ip thermal. I used spoilers to pin the glider then rolled to about 50ft of the hanger taxiway. Marvin said I only needed one more flight to complete the exam.

We towed the glider back to to the approach end of the runway and hooked up for a tow. I called out the rope break procedures at 50 feet increments. At 200 feet we were still over the departure end of the runway so I delayed about 3 seconds before I turned. I deployed the spoilers full as I turned Marvin said to land as close to the hangars as I could I touched down at the hangar taxiway so I closed the spoilers and rolled back to the approach end of the runway. We pushed off to allow the Pawnee to land and hooked back up. Marvin said the test is over but asked me to do a pattern tow and stop as close to the hangars as I could. We hooked up and started a normal tow and at 400 feet Marvin asked what I would do if the rope broke. I pointed out that the airport was just behind us and I would do a normal pattern. I released at 2,700ft and did a normal pattern and landing stopping at the middle of the taxiway in front of the hangars.

The training I received in the end was very complete and safe. I spent too much time during the summer thermalling when I should have been working on basic flying skills. If I did it like the check ride and combined thermalling with check ride skills my flying skills would have returned faster. Despite being a add on rating I hadn't flown for about 2.5 years so my flying skills were rusty.

It makes sense to fly with the same instructor initially for both new students and add on ratings. Switching instructors all the time requires the instructor and the student coming up to speed with each other despite the efficient training records online. This is mostly a perception problem with the student since there isn't a financial penalty for more flights with an instructor. By the time a student solo's they are pretty close to being ready to take the check ride.

With all the waiting during training it would make sense for students to study with each other while they wait for their next flight.
